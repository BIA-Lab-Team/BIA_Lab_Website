The Grand Challenge

Biology is where computing was in the 1950s

We can observe extraordinary phenomena. We cannot yet read them, predict them, or engineer them at will. That is the gap BIA Lab exists to close.

The Scientific Gap

We are missing the abstractions

Cells communicate across tissue. Organisms self-organise from a single egg. Tumours hijack signalling networks with remarkable precision. We can observe all of this, but we lack the computational abstractions to understand, predict, or engineer these systems at will.

Biology currently has no equivalent of operating systems, programming languages, or information architecture. We don't yet have the right abstractions for what cells actually compute. BIA Lab exists to build them.
Why Now

This field is only now becoming possible

Three revolutions have converged. None alone would have been sufficient. Together, they make a new kind of science possible. BIA's timing here is deliberate, not accidental.

Revolution 1

AI has transformed what we can see in data

Foundation models and self-supervised learning now extract biological structure from images at a scale impossible before. The bottleneck is no longer seeing. It is knowing what to look for.

Revolution 2

Imaging has broken through the resolution barrier

Light-sheet microscopy, expansion microscopy, and live multiplexed imaging let us observe single cells in full 3D context, over time, at subcellular resolution. That is the scale where biological information processing actually happens.

Revolution 3

Spatial biology has changed what a dataset means

Spatially-resolved transcriptomics and 3D multiplexed tissue profiling produce data with genuine biological structure: maps of how cells communicate, organise, and decide. The raw material now exists.

Our Foundation

We are not starting from zero

We have already built foundational tools used by researchers worldwide, covering 3D cell segmentation, surface parameterisation, collective motion analysis, and quantitative phenomics. These are the building blocks.

From tools to theory

The tools exist. What we are now pursuing is the deeper question: what is the logic that connects them? What are the computational primitives of biological organisation (the equivalent of data structures, control flow, and information channels) that will let us read, and eventually write, living systems as fluently as we write software?

That is the transition from tool-building to theory-building. From describing what cells do to understanding what cells compute.

If We Succeed

What the world looks like in ten years

This is the target. The reason it is worth pursuing is precisely because it would matter enormously if it came true.

5 years

The software built here becomes a standard toolkit for biological representation learning, used in labs studying development, cancer, immunity, and synthetic biology. The abstractions become part of how computational biologists think about cells.

10 years

A shared computational language for cellular organisation exists, the way TCP/IP exists for networks. Designing a tissue response in simulation before testing in biology becomes a real workflow, not a thought experiment.

For biology

We move from a science that catalogues what cells do to one that understands what cells compute. Therapeutic design, developmental engineering, and synthetic biology become more like engineering and less like guesswork.

For the field

A new discipline exists at the intersection of information theory, dynamical systems, and cell biology. The people who built it are remembered the way early computer scientists are: the ones who found the right abstractions.
