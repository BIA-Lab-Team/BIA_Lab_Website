Help Define the Computational Architecture of Life

The BIA Lab is a new kind of computational biology laboratory. The people who join now aren't just members — they are founding scientists of a field that is only now becoming possible.

We are looking for people who want to develop the Computational Architecture of Living Systems.
The Scientific Gap

Biology is where computing was in the 1950s

We can observe extraordinary phenomena — cells communicating across tissue, organisms self-organising from a single egg, tumours hijacking signalling networks — but we lack the computational abstractions to understand, predict, and engineer these systems at will.

Biology currently has no equivalent of operating systems, programming languages, or information architecture. We don't yet have the right abstractions for what cells actually compute. BIA Lab exists to build them.
Why Now

This field is only now becoming possible

Three revolutions have converged. None of them alone would have been sufficient. Together, they make a new kind of science possible — and make BIA's timing deliberate rather than accidental.

Revolution 1

AI has transformed what we can see in data

Foundation models, self-supervised learning, and large-scale representation learning have made it possible to extract structure from biological images at a scale and resolution that was simply not achievable before. The bottleneck is no longer seeing — it is knowing what to look for.

Revolution 2

Imaging has broken through the resolution barrier

Light-sheet microscopy, expansion microscopy, and live multiplexed imaging now let us observe single cells in full three-dimensional context, over time, at subcellular resolution. Biology is finally visible at the scale where its information processing actually happens.

Revolution 3

Spatial biology has changed what a dataset means

Spatially-resolved transcriptomics, 3D multiplexed tissue profiling, and single-cell phenomics now produce data with genuine biological structure — not just molecular snapshots, but maps of how cells communicate, organise, and decide. The raw material for a computational architecture now exists.

We are not starting from zero

We have already built foundational tools that are used by researchers worldwide — for 3D cell segmentation, surface parameterisation, collective motion analysis, and quantitative phenomics. These are the building blocks.

What we are now pursuing is the deeper question: what is the logic that connects them? What are the computational primitives of biological organisation — the equivalent of data structures, control flow, and information channels — that will let us read and eventually write living systems as fluently as we write software?

If We Succeed

What the world looks like in ten years

Most of what follows is not guaranteed. It is the target. The reason it's worth pursuing is precisely because it would matter enormously if it came true.

5 years

The software you build here becomes a standard toolkit for biological representation learning — used in labs studying development, cancer, immunity, and synthetic biology. The abstractions you develop become part of how computational biologists think about cells.

10 years

A shared computational language for cellular organisation exists — the way TCP/IP exists for networks, or C exists for systems programming. Designing a tissue response in simulation before testing it in biology becomes a real workflow, not a fantasy.

For biology

We move from a science that catalogues what cells do to one that understands what cells compute. Therapeutic design, developmental engineering, and synthetic biology all become more like engineering and less like guesswork.

For you

Your name is on the foundational papers, tools, and ideas of a new discipline — not as a contributor to an established field, but as one of the people who defined how the field thinks.

Who We're Looking For

Mindset over background

We welcome people from any quantitative field — biology, physics, mathematics, engineering, computer science. What we are looking for is the kind of person who, when told something can't be done, asks why not? — and then goes and figures it out. People who are energised by problems that don't have a solution yet, who don't wait for permission to try, and who treat "we don't know" as an invitation rather than a dead end.

You want to invent, not just apply. You reach for first principles when a tool doesn't exist yet, and find that more interesting than using one that does.

You are drawn to hard, messy data. Terabyte-scale imaging, biological noise, and incomplete ground truth excite rather than deter you.

You care about biological meaning. The computation is a means — understanding how cells encode and transmit decisions across space and time is the end.

You finish things. Ideas are cheap; tools that thousands of researchers use are not. You take satisfaction in making something real and releasable.

Equally Important

BIA is probably not for you if…

We'd rather you know this now than six months in. The environment that makes BIA exciting for some people is genuinely wrong for others.

This is probably not the right fit if you…

  • Want a narrowly scoped project with a clear, pre-defined outcome
  • Prefer incremental refinement of established methods over inventing new ones
  • Are uncomfortable with ambiguity or frequently changing research directions
  • Are looking for a lab with a large, established team and deep institutional inertia
  • Find open-ended questions more stressful than motivating
  • Prefer applying existing frameworks to novel data rather than questioning the frameworks themselves

None of these are flaws. They describe many excellent scientists. They just describe a different kind of scientific environment than we are building.

Process

How to apply

We keep first contact simple. Tell us who you are and what you want to work on — we will ask for more if the fit looks right.

1

Read our papers and imagine what you could build here

Explore our research. Ask yourself: where could you push the needle? How could the tools we have already built become the scaffold for something nobody has done yet?

2

Send us two things

CV and a 1-page research vision — what you want to work on and why BIA is the right place to do it. That is all we need to start a conversation. We will ask for referees, research statements, and other materials only after an interview.

3

Use the form or email directly

Submit below, or email felix.y.zhou@vanderbilt.edu with subject [Position] – Application. Strong candidates are contacted within two weeks.

4

Interview

A 45-minute conversation to see if there is genuine mutual fit. Finalists are invited for an on-site visit.
