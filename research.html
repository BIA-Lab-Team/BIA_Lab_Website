Our Projects

We ask how biological systems encode, transmit, and decode information across scales — from single molecules to whole tissues.

Spatial Signaling

Reconstructing signaling architecture from spatial transcriptomics

Modern spatial transcriptomics allows us to measure gene expression while retaining tissue coordinates. We develop graph-based methods to infer directionality and coupling strength of cell–cell communication across tissue sections, identifying signaling hubs and gradients that control cell-fate decisions.

Spatial transcriptomics Graph algorithms Gaussian processes Python

Temporal Dynamics

Oscillatory gene expression and developmental timing

The vertebrate segmentation clock generates periodic pulses of gene expression that time the formation of body segments. We combine live single-cell imaging, optogenetics, and stochastic differential equation models to understand how noise, coupling, and external inputs shape clock precision and entrainability.

Live imaging Optogenetics SDEs / ODEs Bayesian inference

Information Theory

Information-theoretic limits of biological signaling

Using Shannon's framework, we quantify how much information signaling pathways can transmit about their inputs. We compute mutual information and channel capacity from single-cell data, revealing fundamental trade-offs between speed, fidelity, and energy expenditure in cell communication.

Mutual information Channel capacity Single-cell data InfoSig toolkit

Network Architecture

Design principles of gene regulatory networks

We interrogate the topology of regulatory networks to understand how feedback, feed-forward, and modularity contribute to signal processing robustness. Using simulations and evolutionary analysis, we identify network motifs that are enriched in signaling systems and explain their functional advantages.
